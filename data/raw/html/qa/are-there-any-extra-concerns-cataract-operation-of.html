  • Are there any extra concerns for a cataract operation of the "good eye" from a person with amblyopia?

    JUL 13, 2016

    Question:

    Are there any extra concerns for a cataract operation of the "good eye" from a person with amblyopia? (extra possible complications, choice of lens, outcome, etc.)


    Answer:

    The first and foremost concern is safety. Cataract surgery is about 98% (or more) safe and effective. But complications can and do occur. These can be minimal to severe, with blindness occurring in less than one in ten thousand cataract surgeries. If severe complications occur in a one-eyed patient it can be terrible.

    In my career, I performed cataract surgery on hundreds of patients with severe problems in the other eye such as amblyopia (poor vision in an eye that did not develop normal sight during early childhood), advanced macular disease and even glass eyes. I thank the lord that all of them went well and all of these patients were happy with their vision after surgery. I usually told these patients much of what I have just told you and more. I would tell them I would not do cataract surgery on their only good eye until they almost grabbed me by the shirt and tie and told me that I must do the surgery. In other words, it’s best to wait to do surgery until it is interfering in a major way with your life since the risk to your good eye is real, even though low.

    It's important to ask your surgeon about patching the operated eye after cataract surgery. Most of us would not use a patch on any patient after cataract surgery, but you need to check on this. Additionally, vision after cataract surgery can be blurry for a few hours to a few days. You would do well to have a loved one or friend live with you during your first few days after surgery. Consider preparing and freezing the meals you will need for a few days after surgery. You will also need someone to drive you to and from your ophthalmologist for your first, and possibly second, post op follow-up exams until your ophthalmologist tells you it is OK for you to drive. Be certain to fully understand all of your after-surgery instructions, do’s and don’ts, etc. before the procedure. Finally, you reference choice of the intraocular lens: this requires consultation with your ophthalmologist and some self-education on your part. I believe that all of the IOL options are good and offer choices of convenience relative to near, intermediate and distance vision with or without eyeglasses. These options are too lengthy for this discussion.

    I understand what you are going through and I sincerely wish you the very best of luck, and in my heart, I know you will love your new vision.


    Answered By: Wayne F Bizer DO

    Surgery Cataracts
